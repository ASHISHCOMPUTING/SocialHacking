If Face Then That

Background

One of the most salient objects in our day-to-day life is the human face. Faces are so important that the impairment of our face-processing ability is seen as a disorder, called prosopagnosia, while unconsciously seeing faces where there are none is a common experience called pareidolia.

As early as the 1960s, computers were aiding humans with face identification, or recognition: finding a face in a database of faces. In the late 1980s this turned into basic face detection: finding a face in an image. Since the early 2000s face tracking in video has become common place, with companies like Affectiva and Emotient capitalizing on expression analysis to identify marketing strategies that emotionally resonate with viewers. State of the art algorithms can now discern "genuine pain" from "fake pain" 85% of the time (humans score closer to 50%).

Academic researchers working with face tracking work with databases on the order of 10k (2007) faces to 100k (2014) faces. For comparison, the NSA collects 55k faces per day, and Android users alone take 93 million selfies taken per day.

Preparation

The goal is to assemble a collection of sounds for two participants to use in a conversation with each other. You may assemble the same soundboard for both of them, or prepare two separate soundboards.

  1. Click "Allow" when the page asks to use your microphone.
  2. Click the red record button to record a sound.
  3. Click the stop button to finish recording the sound.
  4. Once the sound is recorded, you can click anywhere in the square to play the sound.
  5. Double-click the text to change the name of the sound.
  6. Double-click the trash can to delete the sound and re-record.
  7. The data will automatically sync with a server. Copy and paste the URL to come back to this soundboard.

Usage

To use someone's soundboard, you should only click on the middle of the rectangles to play the sounds. Don't delete or rename any sounds.
